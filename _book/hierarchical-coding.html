Factors can optionally be expressed as part of a hierarchy by using semi-colons. If you want to use a different separator like â€œ>â€ or â€œ/â€, you can change that in the settings.

31.7 Smart simplification

A weakness of the above approach is already visible in the example, so simplification goes one step further. In many cases we may be happy to see all the factors â€œrolled upâ€ to the top level but in the above example, â€œHealth behaviour; hand washingâ€ dominates. â€œHealth behaviourâ€ itself is only mentioned once in the raw data, and there is only one other mention of it as a higher-level component, in relation to vaccinations. What can we do about this?

Another way to simplify a causal map is just to hide factors and/or links which are mentioned less frequently. Often it is hard to view a full causal map, especially because of the presence of very many of what we could call â€œtiddlersâ€: factors with just one or two mentions. But this is unsatisfactory because it throws away information. This wonâ€™t help us, but can we combine the two approaches?

A smarter approach is achievable with this simple algorithm: step by step, â€œroll upâ€ the least frequent factor by one level, and stop when the desired level of generality has been reached, i.e.Â when the number of remaining factors is satisfactorily small.

This is an improvement over zooming because it retains detail where it is necessary and removes it where it is not. It will roll up lots of infrequent, granular factors into their parents but only if they are infrequent; if you have a very granular factor like â€œhealth behaviour; hand washingâ€ which actually has a lot of mentions, then it wonâ€™t get rolled up. It will also completely remove infrequent top-level â€œtiddlersâ€ which cannot be rolled up into anything else. It is also welcome because the process can be stopped at any desired stage to get just the right level of detail.

31.8 Simplifying (zooming out, and filtering out less frequent links and factors) in the app

Why would I use this filter? An unfiltered causal map can be quite overwhelming to begin with as it is presenting all the causal links which have been coded, so this filter can be a good place to start. By filtering out links/factors which have only been mentioned once or twice, the map becomes more manageable, easier to make sense of, and displays only the most frequent stories of change/causal pathways.
