But sometimes this can mean oversimplifying your map: there are lower-level factors which are so important and popular that it is unhelpful or even misleading to roll them up into a parent factor. Smart zooming solves that problem.

A weakness of hierarchical zooming is already visible in the example, so simplification goes one step further. In many cases we may be happy to see all the factors â€œrolled upâ€ to the top level but in the above example, â€œHealth behaviour; hand washingâ€ dominates. â€œHealth behaviourâ€ itself is only mentioned once in the raw data, and there is only one other mention of it as a higher-level component, in relation to vaccinations. What can we do about this?

Another way to simplify a causal map is just to hide factors and/or links which are mentioned less frequently. Often it is hard to view a full causal map, especially because of the presence of very many of what we could call â€œtiddlersâ€: factors with just one or two mentions. But this is unsatisfactory because it throws away information. This wonâ€™t help us, but can we combine the two approaches?

A smarter approach is achievable with this simple algorithm: only â€œroll upâ€ a nested factor into a higher-level factor if it has a frequency (simple frequency, or source count) lower than some cut-off value; otherwise, keep it separate.

This is an improvement over zooming because it retains detail where it is necessary and removes it where it is not. It will roll up lots of infrequent, granular factors into their parents but only if they are infrequent; if you have a very granular factor like â€œhealth behaviour; hand washingâ€ which actually has a lot of mentions, then it wonâ€™t get rolled up.
